# Error Patterns Through a Critical Rationalist Lens

## Structural Errors in Reasoning

### Unfalsifiable Claims

Claims structured to be immune from criticism:
- Moving goalposts when challenged
- Vague predictions that fit any outcome
- Conspiracy-theory structure (evidence against = evidence of cover-up)
- Appeal to unobservable mechanisms with no testable consequences

**Test**: What would have to be true for this claim to be false?

### Ad Hoc Modifications

Rescuing a theory from refutation by adding arbitrary qualifications:
- Adding epicycles rather than abandoning the model
- "It would have worked except for X" where X was unpredicted
- Explaining away anomalies without generating new predictions

**Test**: Does the modification make new predictions, or only save the old theory?

### Justificationism

Demanding proof or certainty before accepting criticism:
- "You can't prove that's wrong"
- "That's just your opinion"
- Infinite regress of "but how do you know that?"
- Refusing to update without "sufficient evidence"

**The error**: Beliefs need not be justified to be held; they need only survive criticism.

### Inductivism

Treating accumulated observations as proof:
- "It's always been this way"
- "The data supports this conclusion"
- Confusing correlation with explanation
- Prediction without mechanism

**The error**: No amount of confirming instances proves a theory; what matters is whether it explains and survives criticism.

## Failures of Criticism

### Straw Man Arguments

Criticizing a weaker version of a position:
- Oversimplifying the opposing view
- Attacking implications the holder doesn't accept
- Ignoring the strongest form of the argument

**The requirement**: Always steel-man first. Before any criticism, construct the strongest possible version of the argument - the version a thoughtful, informed proponent would make. Only then proceed to critique. If you cannot articulate a strong version, you do not yet understand the position well enough to criticize it.

### Ignoring Genuine Problems

Dismissing criticism without engaging it:
- "That's a edge case"
- "In practice it works fine"
- Changing the subject to tangential issues
- Ad hominem (criticizing the critic instead of the criticism)

**The requirement**: Every genuine criticism deserves a response - either refutation or modification.

### False Dichotomies

Presenting limited options when more exist:
- "Either A or B" when C, D, E are possible
- "If not my theory, then chaos"
- Excluding creative alternatives

**The error**: Problem-solving often requires inventing new options, not choosing among given ones.

## Explanatory Failures

### Bad Explanations (Easy to Vary)

Explanations that can accommodate any observation:
- "Human nature" as explanation for any behavior
- Post-hoc rationalization
- Explanations with free parameters that can be adjusted arbitrarily
- "It's complicated" as a stopping point

**Test**: Could this explanation be adjusted to fit the opposite observation?

### Instrumentalism

Treating theories as mere prediction tools:
- "It works, who cares why"
- "The model is useful, not true"
- Refusing to engage with explanatory content
- Pragmatism as an excuse to avoid criticism

**The error**: Understanding why something works matters - it's what enables progress.

### Reductionism Without Integration

Explaining away rather than explaining:
- "It's just neurons firing"
- "It's just economics"
- Eliminating higher-level phenomena rather than connecting them

**The requirement**: Good reduction explains how higher-level phenomena emerge, not that they're illusory.

## Social/Rhetorical Errors

### Appeal to Authority

Treating status as evidence:
- "Experts agree"
- "The consensus is"
- "Who are you to question X?"

**The reality**: Arguments stand or fall on their content, not their source. Expertise is a reason to listen carefully, not a reason to stop thinking.

### Appeal to Popularity

Treating widespread belief as evidence:
- "Everyone knows"
- "Most people think"
- "Common sense says"

**The reality**: Common beliefs have often been wrong. Popularity is not a criterion for truth.

### Appeal to Tradition

Treating longevity as evidence:
- "It's always been done this way"
- "Ancient wisdom"
- "Time-tested"

**The reality**: Traditions may encode useful knowledge, but must still be criticizable. Persistence is not proof.

### False Balance

Treating all positions as equally valid:
- "Both sides have a point"
- "The truth is somewhere in the middle"
- "Who's to say who's right?"

**The reality**: Some positions are better than others. Critical preference is possible and necessary.

## Meta-Errors

### Demanding Foundations

Requiring certain starting points before reasoning:
- "What are your first principles?"
- "You have to start somewhere"
- "Without foundations, anything goes"

**The error**: Knowledge is not a building needing foundations but a raft we repair while sailing. All parts are criticizable; none are privileged.

### Relativism

Claiming truth is relative to perspective:
- "That's true for you"
- "Different cultures have different truths"
- "Science is just another narrative"

**The error**: Confuses the sociology of belief (what people believe) with epistemology (what's true). Criticism is possible across frameworks.

### Pessimistic Induction

Concluding we'll always be wrong because we've been wrong before:
- "They thought X was true and were wrong; we'll be wrong too"
- "Science always changes its mind"

**The error**: Conflates "we will have errors" with "we cannot make progress." Our errors become more subtle and our theories more powerful.
