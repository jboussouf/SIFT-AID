# Popperian Epistemology: Core Concepts

## The Central Problem

How does knowledge grow? Not through justification or verification, but through **conjecture and refutation** - bold guesses subjected to severe criticism.

## Foundational Principles

### Falsifiability

A theory is scientific/rational only if it makes predictions that could, in principle, be shown false. Unfalsifiable claims are not necessarily wrong, but they cannot participate in rational discourse because no evidence could ever count against them.

**Key test**: What observation or argument would cause you to abandon this position?

### Critical Preference

We cannot prove theories true, but we can have rational preferences. Prefer theories that:
1. Have survived severe criticism (not just any criticism - tests that could genuinely have failed)
2. Have greater explanatory power (explain more with less)
3. Make bolder, more precise predictions
4. Are more falsifiable (take more risks)

### The Asymmetry of Verification and Falsification

- No amount of confirming instances proves a universal theory
- A single genuine counterexample refutes it
- This asymmetry is the engine of knowledge growth

### Verisimilitude (Truthlikeness)

Though we cannot know if a theory is true, theories can be closer to or further from the truth. We aim to increase verisimilitude through error elimination.

## The Growth of Knowledge

### Problem → Tentative Theory → Error Elimination → New Problem

Knowledge grows through this cycle:
1. Start with a **problem** (not observations)
2. Propose **tentative solutions** (conjectures, theories)
3. Subject them to **severe criticism** (attempted refutations)
4. **Eliminate errors** (refuted theories)
5. Arrive at **new problems** (deeper than the original)

This process never ends. We never arrive at certain truth, only at better problems.

### The Role of Problems

Problems come first, not observations. "Science begins with problems rather than with observations" - we always observe in light of theoretical expectations. The myth of the blank-slate observer collecting neutral facts is incoherent.

## Three Worlds

Popper's ontology distinguishes:

- **World 1**: Physical objects and states
- **World 2**: Mental states, subjective experiences
- **World 3**: Objective knowledge - theories, arguments, problems, as abstract objects

World 3 is autonomous - once created, theories have logical consequences we may not have intended or foreseen. Criticism operates on World 3 objects.

## Rationality as Criticism

### What Rationality Is

Rationality is not a method for arriving at truth. It is an attitude:
- Willingness to listen to criticism
- Willingness to learn from mistakes
- Recognition of one's own fallibility
- Commitment to solving problems through argument rather than force

### What Rationality Is Not

- Certain knowledge
- Justified true belief
- Following rules or methods mechanically
- Requiring foundations or first principles

## Key Anti-Patterns

### Justificationism

The demand that beliefs be justified by evidence or argument. This leads to infinite regress (what justifies the justification?) or arbitrary stopping points. Replace with: holding beliefs tentatively, always open to criticism.

### Inductivism

The belief that theories can be derived from or supported by accumulated observations. No amount of white swans proves all swans are white. Theories are conjectures, not inductions.

### Instrumentalism

Treating theories as mere prediction-making tools with no truth content. This abandons the search for explanations and understanding.

### Essentialism

Seeking the "true nature" or "essence" of things through definitions. Definitions are conventions; what matters is how theories solve problems.

## The Open Society

Rationality extends to social organization:
- Institutions should be criticizable and reformable
- No authority is beyond question
- Piecemeal social engineering over utopian planning
- Tolerance of all except the intolerant
